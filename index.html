The problem
We have no good way to think together at scale
Town halls devolve into shouting matches. Online comment sections reward outrage. Surveys impose the designer's assumptions. And "consultation" processes often exist to tick a box, not to listen.
Meanwhile, communities hold extraordinary collective wisdom — about what matters, what's possible, and what they'd actually commit to building together. We just lack the infrastructure to surface it.
Agora is that infrastructure. Built on the same algorithmic foundations as Polis — the platform behind Taiwan's vTaiwan participatory democracy process — Agora makes the structure of disagreement visible, and helps groups find common ground without forcing false consensus.
Process
Four phases, from dreaming to doing
Agora structures deliberation into phases that mirror how good collective decisions actually get made — expanding before converging, understanding before committing.
01
✦
Dream
Wide-open brainstorm. Everyone submits ideas and votes agree, disagree, or pass. No constraints, no filtering — just the raw material of collective imagination.
02
◈
Shape
The opinion map is revealed. Clusters emerge. Participants see where they sit in the landscape and respond with more nuanced, bridging contributions. The picture gets clearer.
03
◆
Commit
Consensus items become action items. Participants signal what they'll help make happen. Ideas become plans. Plans find people.
04
○
Reflect
What worked? What surprised us? The conversation becomes a record and a reference — seeding the next iteration of collective thinking.
Under the hood
Faithful implementation of proven methods
Agora implements the core algorithms described in the Polis research paper, adapted for phased deliberation and community-scale deployment.
Dimensionality Reduction
PCA via Power Iteration
Votes form a matrix. Principal component analysis projects participants into a 2D opinion space — a map of where people stand, computed from how they actually voted.
Clustering
K-Means with Silhouette Selection
Opinion groups emerge automatically. The algorithm tests multiple cluster counts and selects the one that best captures the real structure of disagreement.
Consensus Detection
Group-Aware Consensus Metric
Consensus isn't just majority agreement. Agora computes the product of agreement across all groups — protecting minority dissent and surfacing genuine cross-group common ground.
Bridging
Cross-Cluster Agreement Scoring
The most valuable statements are those that unite otherwise-separated groups. Bridging scores identify the ideas with the power to transcend tribal boundaries.
Missing Data
Sparsity-Aware Correction
Not everyone votes on every statement. The √(C/Cp) scaling factor corrects for this, preventing under-voters from clumping at the centre of the map.
Comment Routing
Priority-Weighted Distribution
Statements are shown in a semi-random order weighted toward those most likely to build consensus, have high PCA loading, or are new and need exposure.

About
Designed for communities, not committees
Created by
Meaghan
Industrial Designer Civic Tech Collaborative Futures
I'm an industrial designer who believes that the most important thing we can design is how we make decisions together. The tools we use to think collectively shape what we're capable of imagining — and currently, those tools are badly broken.
Agora started as a question: what if planning a community festival could double as a prototype for participatory democracy? I was organising an event at my family's regional property — a celebration with friends and extended community — and I wanted to know: could we use the process of planning it together to test whether these deliberation tools actually work?
The answer, it turns out, is yes. When you give people a simple interface to share ideas and vote, and then show them the landscape of where everyone stands — clusters form, consensus emerges, and people get excited about what they're building together. Joy turns out to be a powerful design principle for democracy.
The longer-term vision is civic deliberation infrastructure for Australia. Taiwan's vTaiwan process demonstrated that digital tools can genuinely connect citizen deliberation to legislative outcomes. Australia doesn't have an equivalent yet. I'm interested in building toward one — starting with real communities, real stakes, and real joy.
We will all die in the not too distant future. What we build together in the meantime is what matters.
